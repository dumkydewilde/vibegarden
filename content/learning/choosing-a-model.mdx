Open any model picker (including the one in The Gardener's sidebar) and you face a wall of names and version numbers. Good news: choosing well is much simpler than the leaderboard drama suggests.

## The three things that actually differ

Every model sits somewhere on three axes:

- **Quality**: how well it handles hard, multi-step, or subtle work.
- **Speed**: how fast the answer streams back.
- **Price**: what you pay per million tokens, which ranges from free to a hundred times that.

You rarely get all three. The trick is knowing which one your task needs. Summarizing a newsletter needs none of the top-end quality; planning a tricky database schema might.

## Benchmarks, and why to hold them loosely

Benchmarks are standardized test sets: math problems, coding tasks, exam questions. They are how new models announce themselves ("state of the art on...") and they are genuinely useful for a rough ranking. But hold them loosely, for two reasons. First, models are sometimes accidentally (or not so accidentally) trained on the test questions, like a student who saw the exam beforehand. Second, and more important: a benchmark measures the benchmark's task, not yours. A model that aces competition math can still be mediocre at writing warm Dutch emails.

A more honest signal is revealed preference: what do people actually use when they pay per token? [OpenRouter's rankings](https://openrouter.ai/rankings) show exactly that, live usage across hundreds of models, sliceable by use case. It is one of the most useful pages in AI, because nobody keeps paying for a model that does not work for them.

Notice the spread: the cheapest paid option here costs about thirty times less than the most expensive one. That is exactly why the procedure below starts cheap. For live prices and the full list, [OpenRouter's model page](https://openrouter.ai/models) is the place.

## A procedure that beats reading reviews

1. **Start with a cheap, fast, popular model.** Anything near the top of the OpenRouter rankings in the low price range is a fine default.
2. **Keep a tiny personal test.** Save five examples of your real task: your actual emails, your actual data. When you consider a new model, run those five. Ten minutes, and it tells you more than any leaderboard.
3. **Upgrade only on visible failure.** If the cheap model garbles your task, step up one tier and rerun your five examples. Most tasks plateau early: past a certain point, a pricier model just gives you the same answer more expensively.

## What makes a model good

Here is the uncomfortable secret behind all the leaderboard drama: for most real tasks, which model you pick matters less than what you *give* it. A mid-tier model that can see your actual data, knows your definitions, and has the right tools will beat a flagship model working from a vague one-line prompt, almost every time.

So when a result disappoints, the fix is usually not "upgrade the model." Ask instead:

- **Did it have the right context?** Could it see the document, the data, the background it needed, or was it forced to guess?
- **Did it have the right tools?** An agent that can look things up does not have to remember them. A model asked to compute in its head will lose to a cheap model with a calculator tool.
- **Can it handle what you gave it?** This is the part where model quality genuinely shows: better models are better at navigating large piles of context and at using tools correctly, not just at being "smarter" in the abstract.

That last point is worth internalizing: modern model quality is mostly about *handling context and tools well*, and your job is mostly about *providing* them well. [Creating the right context](/learning/creating-the-right-context) shows how far a simple folder of notes can take you, which is further than most model upgrades.

## Try it right here

The Gardener's model picker exists precisely for this. Ask the same question to a free model and a top-tier model and compare. For "what should I cook with leftover courgette?" you will struggle to tell them apart. For "design my volunteer-scheduling tool" you will start to see the difference. Building that intuition, this task is easy, that task is hard, is the real skill, and it transfers to every model that comes out next month.
