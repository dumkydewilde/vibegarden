Voice is underused in hobby projects, which is a shame: it is often the difference between a tool people try once and a tool they actually use. Grandma will not type into a form; she will absolutely talk to one. Voice work splits into three parts: getting speech in, producing speech out, and the newer trick of skipping text entirely.

## Voice in: transcription

Speech-to-text is a solved problem, and that sentence would have been false five years ago. Models like OpenAI's [Whisper](https://github.com/openai/whisper) transcribe messy, accented, multilingual audio, including Dutch, including your uncle's Brabants, nearly perfectly and nearly for free.

This unlocks a pattern worth memorizing: **voice notes are an input format.** Record a rambling two-minute note, transcribe it, then hand the text to an LLM to structure: shopping list, diary entry, meeting minutes, ideas sorted by topic. Talking is three times faster than typing, and the LLM cleans up the rambling. Half the "I wish I had a tool for..." ideas in this workshop can be built as: record, transcribe, extract (see [Extracting meaning from text](/learning/extracting-meaning-from-text)).

## Voice out: text-to-speech

The other direction has crossed the uncanny valley too. Two names to know:

- **[ElevenLabs](https://elevenlabs.io)**: the best-known, with rich expressive voices, many languages, and voice cloning: a minute of someone's speech is enough to make a voice that reads anything in their tone. (Clone only voices you have permission to clone.)
- **[Cartesia](https://cartesia.ai)**: built for speed. Its voices answer fast enough for live conversation, which matters the moment you build anything interactive: an assistant that pauses four seconds before answering feels broken, one that answers instantly feels alive.

Project-shaped ideas: a bedtime-story machine that reads your kids' own adventures aloud, a recipe reader for floury hands, your newsletter as a private podcast.

## Voices live in latent space too

Here is the connecting idea: just as words become coordinates on a map of meaning ([Tokens, embeddings, and latent space](/learning/tokens-embeddings-latent-space)), a voice can be reduced to coordinates that capture how it sounds: pitch, timbre, accent, pace. Voice cloning is finding your voice's spot on that map and speaking new sentences from there. And because speech and text can share one map, the newest "speech-native" models skip transcription entirely: they hear and speak directly, which is how they can whisper, laugh, or respond to your tone rather than just your words.

## Where to start

Simplest first project: pick one direction. Input-only (voice notes to structured text) needs just a transcription step bolted onto ideas you already have. Output-only (read my thing aloud) is one API call to ElevenLabs or Cartesia. Full conversations, both directions live, are a real step up: save those for project two.
