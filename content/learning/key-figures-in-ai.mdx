You do not keep up with AI by reading AI news; there is too much of it, and most of it is noise. The trick is to follow a handful of people who build things, write clearly, and change their minds in public. Below are two short lists: people worth following right now, and the foundational figures whose ideas everything runs on. Names, not homework: pick one or two and ignore the rest.

## Worth following today

| Who | Why follow them |
| --- | --- |
| **Andrej Karpathy** | Former OpenAI founding member and Tesla AI director, now the field's best teacher. His [YouTube lectures](https://www.youtube.com/@AndrejKarpathy) build a GPT from scratch in front of you, and he coined "vibe coding", the thing this workshop does all day. |
| **Simon Willison** | Blogs at [simonwillison.net](https://simonwillison.net) almost daily about actually using LLMs: what works, what breaks, what surprised him. The closest thing the field has to a public lab notebook, and refreshingly free of hype. |
| **Boris Cherny** | Created Claude Code at Anthropic and posts at [x.com/bcherny](https://x.com/bcherny). Worth following for how he thinks about working *with* coding agents rather than just prompting them, which is the skill this whole garden is about. |
| **Ethan Mollick** | Wharton professor writing the [One Useful Thing](https://www.oneusefulthing.org) newsletter for people who are not programmers. If a friend asks "what does AI mean for my job?", this is the person to point them at. |
| **swyx (Shawn Wang)** | Runs the [Latent Space](https://www.latent.space) podcast and newsletter, and coined "AI engineer" for the job that sits between using models and inventing them. Good radar for what builders are excited about this month. |

A reasonable diet is two of these: one practical writer (Willison or Mollick) and one technical teacher (Karpathy). When a new name keeps showing up in their posts, that is your cue to add it.

## The foundations

These people mostly do not need following; they need knowing, because their work explains why the last few years happened at all.

- **Geoffrey Hinton, Yann LeCun, and Yoshua Bengio** kept neural networks alive through the decades when the field considered them a dead end, and shared the 2018 Turing Award for it (Hinton added a Nobel Prize in 2024). They now disagree loudly about how dangerous AI is, which is itself worth watching: the founders of the field do not agree on where it goes.
- **Fei-Fei Li** built ImageNet, the giant labeled photo collection that proved a point people had missed: the data matters as much as the algorithm. The 2012 breakthrough that started the deep learning boom happened on her dataset.
- **The eight authors of ["Attention Is All You Need"](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762)** wrote the 2017 paper introducing the transformer, the T in GPT and the architecture behind every model in [What is an LLM, really?](/learning/what-is-an-llm). Nearly all of them have since left Google to start AI companies, which tells you what they thought they had.
- **Ilya Sutskever** connects the two eras: he was on the 2012 ImageNet-winning team and later, as OpenAI's chief scientist, championed the unreasonable-sounding bet that simply making models bigger would keep making them smarter. So far, he has mostly been right.

## Why names matter

Knowing who is talking turns a firehose into a conversation. When Karpathy posts a new lecture or Willison writes up an experiment, you are hearing from someone with skin in the game, not a headline optimized for clicks. And when you are choosing what to build with, the same filter applies: [Choosing a model](/learning/choosing-a-model) is easier when you recognize the people behind each lab.
