## What it is

An image maker adds pictures your project could never have otherwise: illustrations for the bedtime-story machine, sprites for your game, a logo for the club, one image per recipe. Diffusion models condense an image out of pure noise, steered by your words, which explains both the magic and the quirks: every generation is different, and "the vibe of a rainy evening" works better than "exactly seven tulips". [Generating images](/learning/generating-images) has the full story.

## When to use it

- Your project needs many images in one style: game assets, story illustrations, one picture per item in a collection.
- The image does not exist and cannot be photographed: your kid's dragon, the club mascot, the house as it might look repainted.
- A picture would make the output feel finished: a generated header per newsletter summary, a cover per playlist.

If you need one image once, that is a chat message, not a building block. It becomes a block when images are produced as part of the flow.

## Setup steps

1. Decide the style in words before generating anything: write five lines describing colors, mood, and medium ("flat illustration, warm autumn palette, thick outlines"). This is your style sheet; paste it into every prompt.
2. Pick your pole: [Midjourney](https://www.midjourney.com) when beauty and atmosphere matter most, Nano Banana (Google's image model, inside [Gemini](https://gemini.google.com)) when you need instructions followed, text rendered, or edits to an existing image.
3. For a recurring character, make a character sheet first: "the same character in 6 poses" in one image, then use that image as a reference in every later prompt.
4. Automate last: most image APIs are one call, so once prompts are reliable, your project can generate images the same way it generates text.

## Options and costs

More than text, less than you fear: images cost a few cents each through an API, and Midjourney is a subscription (around ten euros per month). Prototype in the free tiers of Gemini or ChatGPT first. The real cost is time spent regenerating, which is why the style sheet and character sheet steps come first: consistency is something you set up, not something you get.
