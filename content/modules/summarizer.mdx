## What it is

A summarizer takes text that is too long to read and produces the version you actually wanted: the three decisions from the meeting, the one paragraph that matters from the newsletter, the gist of a 40-message email thread. Transformation tasks like this are the sweet spot of language models, because the input already contains the answer.

## When to use it

- A recurring stream of text arrives faster than you read it: newsletters, minutes, group chats, reports.
- Different readers need different versions: the detailed notes for you, the two-liner for the club members.
- You want structured output from unstructured text: action items with owners, dates pulled into a list.

The recurring part matters. Summarizing one document is a chat message, not a project. A summarizer becomes a project when the same kind of text arrives weekly and the output should land somewhere automatically.

## Setup steps

1. Collect three real examples of the input text and write, by hand, the summary you wish you had received. Those pairs define the job.
2. Write the prompt like a brief to a new assistant: what to keep, what to drop, the exact output format, the tone.
3. Test the prompt on your examples in a plain chat until the output matches what you wrote by hand.
4. Automate the boring part: a small script or workflow that grabs the new text (from mail, a folder, a feed), runs the prompt, and delivers the result to your inbox or a [Google Sheet](/garden/modules/google-sheet).

## Options and costs

Cheap. Summarization works well on inexpensive models, and even a daily summarizer usually costs a few cents per month. Long inputs cost more than long outputs, so the main lever is not sending more text than needed. Start by paying nothing: prove the prompt in the free chat, automate only once the output is reliably useful.
