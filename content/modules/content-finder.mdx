## What it is

A content finder checks sources on the web for new items, decides which ones you would care about, and brings them to you: new second-hand listings matching your search, articles about your niche interest, events in your town, a price drop on the thing you are stalking. The AI part is the judgment: not "everything new", but "the ones worth your attention, with a one-line reason".

## When to use it

- You check the same sites over and over, looking for the same kind of thing.
- The filter is fuzzy: "interesting for our newsletter" or "actually a good deal", not just a keyword match.
- You want a digest, not a firehose: five good finds per week beats fifty notifications.

## Setup steps

1. Name your sources. Two or three sites, feeds, or searches you already check by hand. Fewer, better sources beat many noisy ones.
2. Write down your taste as rules: what makes something a keeper, what disqualifies it. This becomes the model's instructions.
3. Check how each source can be read: many sites have RSS feeds (a machine-readable list of new items), which are the easiest and politest way in. Scraping pages is the fallback.
4. Build the loop: fetch new items on a schedule, let the model score them against your rules, deliver the keepers as a digest (email, chat message, or a row in a [Google Sheet](/garden/modules/google-sheet)).

## Options and costs

Cheap to run, but this block has more moving parts than the others: fetching, filtering, scheduling, delivering. The model calls cost pennies per week at digest scale. Two things to respect: check sites at a friendly pace (once an hour is plenty), and expect scrapers to need occasional repair when sites change. Start with one source and a manual "run it now" button before adding schedules.
